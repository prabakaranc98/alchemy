# Sparse autoencoders and crosscoders

A sparse autoencoder decomposes a model's activations into a large, overcomplete dictionary of sparse and approximately monosemantic features, each tending to respond to a single interpretable property of the input. A crosscoder is a sparse autoencoder trained jointly over two or more models or layers, so that its features inhabit a shared space; this is what makes them candidates for transplant from one model to another.

Within the project this is extraction in its strongest form, and the only route that permits verification of what was transferred: a feature can be named, observed to fire, ablated, and checked for survival after the move. For narrow specialties that interpretability is often worth more than raw performance. The crosscoder is accordingly the first step of the composed recipe — extract with a crosscoder, then map, install, and shape. For an initial experiment, publicly released sparse autoencoders remove the cost of training a dictionary: pairing a model with a same-family dictionary, such as Gemma with Gemma Scope, allows one to proceed directly to selecting and transplanting a feature.

Mechanically, the encoder maps activations to sparse codes and the columns of the decoder are the feature directions, so a capability corresponds to one or a few such columns. A cross-model crosscoder trains a single dictionary to reconstruct the activations of both models, yielding a feature index that means the same thing in each, after which a feature direction from the teacher is mapped into the student's space and installed. Two limitations bear on this. Only some classes of feature transfer, since not every unit is a genuine, transportable mechanism — the identifiability concern of [nonlinear ICA](nonlinear-ica.md). And a genuinely cross-architecture crosscoder is itself an unsolved problem, given differences in tokenisation, depth, and width; for an early experiment a same-family model with a public dictionary is the safer choice, deferring the harder cross-architecture case. The usual autoencoder pathologies — feature splitting, dead features, and the reconstruction-sparsity trade-off — apply throughout.
